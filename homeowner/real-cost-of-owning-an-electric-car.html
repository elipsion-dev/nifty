Electric vehicles promise lower running costs — and they often deliver — but the math is more nuanced than a simple comparison to what you paid at the pump. Charging costs vary dramatically depending on whether you plug in at home overnight or stop at a public DC fast-charger on a road trip. Maintenance is genuinely lighter, but tires wear faster and insurance can run higher. Add in a financed purchase and EV-specific registration fees that many states now levy, and the monthly all-in number looks quite different from the loan payment on its own.

This calculator is built specifically for how EV ownership actually works. Enter your purchase price, down payment, and financing terms alongside your real annual operating costs — home charging, public charging, insurance, maintenance, tires, and fees — and it computes your monthly loan payment, total annual ownership cost, and the true monthly average you should be budgeting. All calculation happens directly in your browser; nothing you enter is uploaded anywhere.

Before you run the numbers, it's worth understanding what drives each input for an EV specifically. The sections below break down every cost category so you can estimate confidently rather than guess.

Financing an EV is not the same as financing any other car

The federal EV tax credit changes the effective purchase price — but how you claim it changes what you actually finance. If you take the credit at point of sale through a dealer who participates in the transfer program, the credit reduces your out-of-pocket cost on the day you sign, which means a lower amount financed and lower monthly payments. If you instead claim the credit when you file your taxes, you pay full purchase price upfront, finance the full amount, and receive the credit months later as a tax reduction. Neither approach is universally better, but the timing matters enormously for cash flow and for what this calculator should show in the purchase price and down payment fields.

Leasing adds another wrinkle: when you lease, the leasing company owns the vehicle and claims the tax credit itself — it may or may not pass that value to you through a lower capitalized cost or monthly payment. The calculator models a financed purchase, so if you're comparing lease versus loan, treat the lease as a separate exercise. For a loan, set your purchase price to whatever you're actually paying after any dealer-applied credits, and enter your true out-of-pocket down payment. The APR and loan term then determine your monthly payment, which the calculator surfaces as the first of its three output numbers.

Charging: the input that replaces fuel

Home charging on a Level 2 circuit is the lowest-cost way to keep an EV powered — you're paying your local utility's residential rate per kilowatt-hour, and you're doing it overnight when rates are often lowest. If you don't already have a Level 2 outlet (240V), installation adds an upfront cost that is separate from ongoing electricity bills, but it's a one-time expense rather than a recurring one. For this calculator's annual charging input, use your estimated yearly electricity spend attributable to the car, not the installation cost.

Public DC fast charging is a different story. Per-kilowatt-hour rates at public fast chargers are substantially higher than home rates, and some networks charge per minute rather than per unit of energy. If you rely heavily on public charging — because you live in an apartment, because you travel frequently, or because your home charging capacity is limited — your annual charging cost can approach what a comparable gas vehicle would spend on fuel. The calculator captures both home and public charging in a single combined input so you can reflect your actual split.

Where EVs cost less — and where they cost more

Maintenance is the category where EVs most reliably outperform their gas counterparts. There are no oil changes, no transmission fluid services, no spark plugs, no timing belts. The powertrain has far fewer moving parts. Regenerative braking — the system that recovers energy when you slow down — reduces wear on your physical brake pads so dramatically that many EV owners replace brake hardware far less often than expected. Budget meaningfully less for maintenance than you would on a gas vehicle of similar age.

Tires are the notable exception. Electric vehicles are heavier than comparable gas cars because of the battery pack, and they deliver instant torque that can accelerate tire wear. Expect to replace tires more frequently than you might on a lighter, slower-to-accelerate vehicle, which is exactly why tires get their own dedicated input in this calculator rather than being folded into a general maintenance field.

Insurance tends to run higher on EVs. Battery packs are expensive to repair or replace after a collision, and not every body shop has the equipment or certified technicians to work on high-voltage systems, which concentrates repairs among fewer shops and keeps labor rates elevated. Shop multiple insurers; rates vary significantly by carrier for EVs.

Registration and annual fees can also be higher. Many states have introduced dedicated EV road-use fees — separate from standard registration — to offset the loss of gas-tax revenue from drivers who never stop at a pump. These fees vary widely by state and can add a meaningful amount to your annual fixed costs. Check your state's DMV schedule and fold that figure into the registration/taxes/fees input.

Reading the three outputs

The calculator returns three numbers. Your monthly loan payment is the standard amortized payment based on the amount financed, APR, and term — this is what you'll owe the lender each month. Your annual ownership cost adds your yearly operating inputs to the annualized loan cost, giving you the full picture of what the vehicle costs to own each year. The monthly all-in average divides that annual total by twelve so you can compare against your actual monthly budget. The gap between your loan payment alone and the all-in monthly average is the number that tends to surprise new EV owners most — it represents insurance, charging, tires, and fees expressed in the same monthly cadence as your car payment.

Frequently asked questions

Should I include my Level 2 charger installation cost anywhere in this calculator?

The calculator is designed for recurring annual costs, so a one-time installation expense doesn't fit cleanly into any field. One practical approach is to divide the installation cost by the number of years you expect to own the vehicle and add that amortized amount to your annual home charging input. It's an approximation, but it gives you a more honest all-in picture than ignoring the cost entirely — especially if installation runs into the thousands of dollars for electrical panel upgrades.

The calculator doesn't show a federal tax credit line — where does that factor in?

Intentionally. The credit's value, eligibility, and timing depend on your income, the specific vehicle, the model year, and whether you take it at point of sale or at filing — variables that change frequently and that only you can verify for your situation. Rather than hard-coding a credit that may not apply to you, the calculator puts that decision in your hands: if you're receiving a dealer-applied credit that reduces your out-of-pocket price, enter the reduced purchase price. If you're claiming at filing, enter the full price you're financing. The credit affects your inputs; it doesn't need its own output line.

Why should I enter tires separately from maintenance and repairs?

Because for EVs, tire wear is genuinely elevated and predictable in a way that most other EV maintenance is not. If tires were bundled into a general maintenance input, the natural tendency would be to borrow a low EV maintenance estimate from somewhere online — which is accurate for things like oil changes and fluids — and then undercount tire replacement entirely. Separating tires forces you to estimate that cost deliberately. Budget based on the tire size your vehicle takes, the typical replacement interval at the mileage you drive, and current pricing for EV-rated tires, which often carry a load rating premium over standard passenger tires.
