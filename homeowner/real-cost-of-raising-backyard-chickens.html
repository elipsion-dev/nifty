Backyard chickens have real appeal: fresh eggs, a low-key hobby, and a connection to where food comes from. But the ongoing expenses surprise many first-time flock owners. Feed, bedding, healthcare, and the small but steady stream of replacement supplies add up month after month, year after year, regardless of how many eggs your hens are laying.

This calculator focuses on the recurring annual costs of keeping a backyard flock. Enter your own numbers for feed, bedding, health products, supplies, and electricity, and you will see your annual total, a monthly average, and a five-year projection. What it does not include — by design — is the cost of building or buying a coop and run, or the purchase price of the birds themselves. Those are one-time upfront expenses that vary enormously and deserve their own separate budget.

If you are hoping chickens will pay for themselves through egg savings, the honest answer is that they rarely do. A typical carton of store-bought eggs is still far cheaper than the cost per dozen once you account for the inputs below. That does not mean keeping chickens is a bad decision — for many people the enjoyment is worth the cost — but going in with clear numbers makes the experience far less frustrating.

What goes into the annual cost

Each input field in the calculator represents a real, recurring expense. Understanding what drives each one helps you enter realistic numbers and spot where you might reduce costs over time.

Annual feed

Feed is the single largest line item for almost every backyard flock owner. Layer pellets or crumble, scratch grain, mealworm treats, and any supplemental kitchen scraps all factor in. The amount you spend depends on flock size, the quality of feed you buy, how much the birds free-range, and seasonal changes in appetite. Prices vary significantly by region and feed brand, so your local store is the right reference point.

Annual bedding and litter

Coop bedding — pine shavings, straw, sand, or a combination — needs regular topping off and a full deep clean at least once or twice a year. Run litter follows the same pattern. If you compost the spent bedding you may recover some value, but the material itself is a genuine recurring cost.

Annual health and supplements

Healthy flocks still need preventive care: poultry vitamins, electrolytes during heat stress, diatomaceous earth or other parasite controls, and the occasional vet visit or medication when a bird is ill. Oyster shell and grit are small but consistent purchases. Most flock owners find health costs lower in good years and noticeably higher when disease or injury strikes.

Annual supplies and replacements

Feeders, waterers, nest box pads, heat lamps, bulbs, hardware cloth patches, and general coop hardware all wear out or break over time. This category also includes anything you buy to expand or improve the setup. It is easy to underestimate until you track a full year of small purchases.

Annual coop electricity for heat and light

If your winters are cold, you likely run a heat source in the coop. Supplemental lighting to maintain egg production through short winter days adds to the bill as well. The cost depends on your local electricity rate, the wattage of your equipment, and how many months of the year you run it.

What this calculator does not include

The estimate covers recurring operating costs only. It deliberately excludes the purchase or construction cost of a coop and run, fencing, any initial equipment, and the price of the birds themselves. Those upfront costs can range from a few hundred dollars for a modest DIY setup to several thousand for a prefabricated coop with a large enclosed run. Add those separately when building your full budget.

Frequently asked questions

Do backyard chickens save money on eggs?

For most households, no. When you add up feed, bedding, and other recurring costs, the cost per dozen eggs produced is typically higher than store-bought eggs — sometimes significantly so. Chickens can be worth keeping for reasons beyond economics, but counting on them to lower your grocery bill is usually a mistake.

How many chickens should I budget for?

The inputs in this calculator reflect your whole flock, not per bird, so enter your total annual spend across all your chickens. If you are planning a new flock and estimating in advance, start with local feed prices multiplied by the number of birds and scale the other categories from there. A small flock of three to six hens is a common starting point.

What is not covered in the five-year cost projection?

The five-year figure multiplies your annual inputs by five and does not adjust for inflation or changes in flock size. It also excludes the initial purchase of birds and any coop construction or purchase costs, consistent with the rest of the calculator. Treat it as a rough long-run baseline rather than a precise forecast.
