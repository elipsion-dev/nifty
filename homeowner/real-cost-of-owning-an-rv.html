Financing

Most RVs are financed, and the loans stretch long — frequently 10 to 20 years on larger motorhomes — which keeps the monthly payment palatable while quietly stacking up total interest. RV loan rates typically run higher than mortgage rates and depend on credit, term, and whether the unit is new or used. The trap is depreciation: RVs lose value fast, especially in the first few years, so it is common to owe more than the rig is worth well into the loan. Enter your price, down payment, rate, and term to see the financed cost sitting next to the running costs rather than alone on a dealer worksheet.

Storage

Most suburbs and HOAs prohibit parking an RV on the street or in the driveway long-term, which means renting space for the 40-plus weeks a year you are not traveling. Uncovered outdoor lots are cheapest, covered is more, and indoor climate-controlled storage costs the most but protects seals, tires, and finishes from sun and weather. This is a pure fixed cost — you pay it whether the rig moves or not. Owners with acreage who can store at home erase this line entirely, which is one of the biggest swing factors in the whole budget.

Trip fuel

RVs are among the thirstiest vehicles on the road. A large Class A gas motorhome may return single-digit miles per gallon; a towable behind a pickup is better but still drags that truck's economy down hard. Fuel scales directly with how far and how often you travel, so honest annual mileage is the most important number you'll enter here. For active travelers, fuel can rival or exceed the loan payment. A weekend-camper who stays within a hundred miles will see a fraction of what a cross-country full-timer burns.

Insurance

RV insurance covers the vehicle, liability, and often the contents, and the premium tracks the rig's value, its class, your driving history, and how many months a year you use it. Motorhomes that you drive cost more to insure than towables you tow. Full-timer policies — for people who live in the RV year-round — carry higher premiums than recreational coverage because they function more like home insurance. Rates vary widely by state and insurer, so get a quote on your specific unit rather than guessing from a range.

Maintenance

An RV is a vehicle, a plumbing system, an electrical system, and a set of appliances rolled into one box that flexes and vibrates down the highway. That breadth is why maintenance adds up faster than people expect. Routine items include oil and chassis service, tire replacement (RV tires often age out before they wear out, and a blowout can shred a fender), roof reseals, slide-out seals and mechanisms, water heater and furnace service, and generator upkeep. A rig that sits between trips actually develops problems — seals dry, batteries die, pests move in — so storage and maintenance are linked. Keep a reserve so a failed slide motor or a roof reseal doesn't derail a trip.

  • Roof reseals every few years to prevent water intrusion
  • Tires that age out before the tread wears down
  • Dump-station fees, propane refills, and campground reservation deposits
  • Annual registration, which can be steep for high-value motorhomes
  • Depreciation — not a cash bill, but a real loss if you sell

Campsites

Nightly fees span an enormous range: free dispersed camping on public land, modest national- and state-park sites, and full-hookup resorts that can match a hotel rate. Your annual campsite cost depends almost entirely on how many nights you travel and the style of site you favor. Membership clubs and seasonal or annual passes can cut per-night costs sharply for frequent travelers. Estimate from your realistic trip plans, not the cheapest possible night.

Frequently asked questions

Is it cheaper to rent an RV than to own one?

For occasional travelers, renting is frequently cheaper once you account for storage, insurance, maintenance, and depreciation that you pay all year for a rig used a few weeks. Ownership tends to win only when you travel often enough to spread those fixed costs across many nights. Running this calculator and dividing the annual total by your realistic number of camping nights gives you a per-night figure you can compare directly to a rental quote.

How fast does an RV depreciate?

RVs generally depreciate quickly, often faster than cars, with the steepest drop in the first few years. This calculator doesn't bill depreciation as a monthly cost, but it is a real economic loss the day you sell. Buying a gently used unit lets the first owner absorb that initial plunge, which is why many experienced RVers never buy new.

What's the most expensive part of owning an RV?

For financed buyers it is usually the loan payment; for active travelers, fuel can rival it; and for those in suburbs without home storage, the storage line is a relentless fixed cost. Owners who pay cash and store at home shift their largest expense to maintenance and campsites instead. There is no single answer — which is exactly why adding up your own categories matters.
