Real Cost of Owning a Second Home

A second home promises long weekends at the lake, ski seasons in the mountains, or a standing retreat whenever life gets loud. What it rarely promises is simplicity. Unlike your primary residence, a vacation property sits empty for most of the year — and the bills keep arriving anyway. Before you fall in love with a listing, it helps to see the full carrying cost laid out in one place.

This calculator totals everything that comes due whether you visit or not: your mortgage, property taxes, insurance, utilities, maintenance, HOA dues, and the travel cost to actually use the place. All math runs in your browser — nothing is uploaded or stored. Enter your numbers, and you will see a monthly and annual picture of what the getaway truly costs to keep.

The tool does not model rental income or changes in the home's value over time. It focuses on one thing: what you spend each year to own a home you are not in every day.

Why a second-home mortgage is its own category

Lenders treat a vacation home differently from both a primary residence and an investment property. To qualify as a true second home — rather than an investment property — lenders generally require that you occupy it for some portion of the year and that it not be subject to mandatory rental agreements. That distinction matters because second-home loans typically carry stricter requirements: larger minimum down payments and, in many market conditions, modestly higher rates than you would see on a primary purchase. If you plan to rent the home on a short-term basis for even part of the year, talk to your lender early, because the loan category can shift entirely. The mortgage line in this calculator reflects a straightforward personal-use second home.

Property taxes without the homestead break

Most states and counties offer a homestead exemption — a meaningful reduction in assessed value or tax rate — for the home you live in as your primary residence. A vacation property almost never qualifies. That means you are paying the full assessed rate, and in high-demand resort counties or coastal markets, assessed values can climb quickly. Enter your actual annual tax bill rather than estimating from your primary home's rate; the two numbers can differ more than most buyers expect.

Insurance that reflects a part-time home

Standard homeowner's insurance is priced around a home that is regularly occupied. A second home flips that assumption: it may sit empty for weeks or months at a stretch, which insurers treat as elevated risk for undetected water damage, break-ins, or fire. Premiums are commonly higher than comparable coverage on a primary home, and some carriers require a specific vacancy endorsement or a separate dwelling policy.

Location compounds this. Vacation properties cluster in exactly the spots that carry elevated natural-hazard exposure — coastlines subject to hurricane and storm surge, lakefront land in flood zones, mountain cabins in wildfire corridors, or northern retreats where a frozen pipe can go unnoticed for days. Flood coverage through a separate policy is often mandatory in designated flood zones and wise anywhere near water. Factor your full insurance stack — hazard, flood if applicable, and any umbrella extension — into the annual figure you enter here.

Utilities you pay year-round whether you are there or not

You cannot simply disconnect service when you leave. The heat stays on at a minimum temperature to prevent frozen pipes. The electric account stays active for the sump pump, security system, and refrigerator. Internet and security monitoring may run on monthly subscriptions. What you pay at a vacation property is a floor charge every single month, and a higher charge during the weeks you are actually there. Your annual utility figure should reflect both, not just the peak-use months.

Maintenance and HOA: paying others to manage what you cannot see

At your primary home, you notice when the gutters need clearing or the lawn gets long. At a second home, you rely on someone else to notice — and to fix it. Lawn care, snow removal, pool service, pest control, and seasonal opening and closing costs all land on your ledger precisely because you are not present to handle them yourself. Condo or resort communities add HOA dues on top of that, often covering exterior maintenance, amenity upkeep, and shared infrastructure. A realistic maintenance budget for any home is meaningful; for one you are not watching closely, it tends to run higher.

Travel cost: the expense that only exists because it is a getaway

Every trip to your second home costs money to execute. Flights, fuel, tolls, and ground transportation add up across a year of visits. If the property is a long drive, you are looking at that cost multiplied by every trip your household makes. It is easy to leave travel off a property cost analysis because it feels like a lifestyle expense rather than a housing expense — but it is a direct consequence of owning a home you have to travel to reach. Include an honest annual estimate so the total reflects what the experience actually demands.

What this calculator does not tell you

Home values rise and fall, and a well-located vacation property may appreciate meaningfully over the years you own it. That potential upside is real, and many second-home owners point to it as part of their reasoning. This tool does not model appreciation or depreciation — it focuses purely on the annual cost of ownership so you can compare that cost honestly against the value the property delivers to your life. The financial picture is one input. Whether the getaway is worth it is a question only you can answer.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a second-home loan and an investment-property loan?

Lenders use occupancy intent to draw the line. A second home is a property you plan to use personally for at least part of the year; an investment property is purchased primarily to generate rental income. The distinction affects your down payment requirement, your interest rate, and the underwriting standards you need to meet. Second-home loans generally require a larger down payment than a primary residence but less than a full investment-property loan. If you intend to rent the home for significant portions of the year, your lender may reclassify it as an investment property, which changes the loan terms — so it is worth having that conversation before you make an offer.

Why is insurance typically more expensive on a vacation home than on a similar primary residence?

Insurers price risk based on how quickly a problem is likely to be discovered and addressed. A primary home has someone in it most days; a vacation property may be empty for extended stretches, meaning a slow leak, a pest infestation, or storm damage can go undetected and worsen considerably before anyone notices. That delayed-discovery risk raises premiums. On top of that, many of the locations people choose for vacation homes — waterfronts, mountains, forested areas — carry elevated natural-hazard exposure, which pushes costs higher still. Flood and wildfire risk in particular often require separate policies or endorsements that add to the total insurance bill.

How do you decide whether a second home is "worth it" given the carrying cost?

There is no universal answer, but the carrying-cost figure this calculator produces gives you a concrete starting point for the question. Divide your total annual cost by the number of nights you realistically expect to use the property in a year. That per-night figure becomes a useful reference: you can weigh it against what comparable accommodations would cost you, consider the intangible value of having a consistent place that feels like yours, and think about how often your actual usage tends to drift below your optimistic projections. Many owners find that usage drops after the first few years as novelty fades and life gets busy — building a realistic visit estimate, rather than an aspirational one, leads to a more honest decision.
