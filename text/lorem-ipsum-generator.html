Why designers still use fake Latin

Placeholder text exists to let people judge a layout without reading it. Real sentences pull attention to their meaning — reviewers start editing the copy instead of evaluating the design. Lorem ipsum's scrambled pseudo-Latin has roughly the same word-length rhythm and letter distribution as natural European text, so a mockup filled with it looks typographically honest while staying semantically invisible. That's the entire trick, and it has worked since typesetters started using it centuries ago.

Where the words come from

The traditional passage traces to a scrambled excerpt of Cicero's De Finibus Bonorum et Malorum (45 BC) — "Neque porro quisquam est qui dolorem ipsum quia dolor sit amet…" — mangled by generations of print shops into the familiar "Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet" opening. This generator draws from that classic vocabulary and assembles fresh sentences each time, with varied sentence lengths and the occasional comma clause, so paragraphs don't repeat in the obvious way copy-pasted blocks do.

Matching the text to the job

Paragraphs are for body copy areas — pick the count that roughly matches the finished content so line-wrapping and page length are realistic. Sentences fill card descriptions, captions, and intro blurbs. Words are for headlines, buttons, and labels, where you need "about four words" rather than a paragraph. Starting with the classic "Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet…" opening is a convention worth keeping — it signals unambiguously to everyone downstream that the text is placeholder, not draft copy.

A word of caution about shipping it

Every designer has a story about lorem ipsum reaching production. Two habits prevent it: generate placeholder in a visibly consistent style (the classic opening helps), and grep for "lorem" as a pre-launch check. If a stakeholder might mistake the mockup for final content, say so on the page itself.

Frequently asked questions

Does lorem ipsum mean anything?

Not anymore. The source passage was real Cicero — a meditation on pain and pleasure — but the standard text is chopped and scrambled beyond translation. Individual words like "dolor" (pain) and "labore" (work) are genuine Latin; the passage as a whole is deliberate nonsense.

How much placeholder text should I use in a mockup?

Match the expected real content as closely as you can estimate — a product description that will be two sentences should be mocked with two sentences, not three paragraphs. Layouts tuned to generous filler have a way of breaking when the real, shorter copy arrives.

Can I get the text with HTML tags?

Yes — check the wrap option and each paragraph comes wrapped in <p>…</p>, ready to paste into a template or CMS field that expects markup.
