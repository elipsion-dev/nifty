Required Minimum Distribution Calculator

Estimate an owner RMD using the IRS Uniform Lifetime Table and a prior year-end balance.

A required minimum distribution starts with two numbers: the account balance at the end of the previous calendar year and an IRS life-expectancy divisor for your age in the distribution year. This calculator keeps that arithmetic visible, then subtracts anything you already withdrew toward the year’s requirement.

It is deliberately limited to an original account owner using the Uniform Lifetime Table. That boundary prevents a quick estimate from silently applying the wrong table to a spouse-beneficiary or inherited-account situation.

The RMD equation

Prior December 31 balance ÷ distribution period = calculated RMD. The distribution period is not your remaining life expectancy in plain language; it is the factor assigned by the IRS table. As age increases, the factor generally decreases and the portion required to be distributed increases.

Which balance belongs in the first box?

Use the retirement account’s fair market value on December 31 immediately before the RMD year. Do not enter today’s balance. Rollovers, outstanding rollovers, transfers, and multiple IRAs can complicate the amount a custodian reports, so reconcile the statement before treating the result as final.

When this calculator must stop

If a spouse more than ten years younger is the sole beneficiary, the IRS Joint Life and Last Survivor Table may apply. Beneficiaries of inherited accounts face separate life-expectancy and ten-year rules. Selecting either situation produces a stop message instead of a misleading dollar amount.

Whether a distribution is due is a separate question

Current federal rules generally start owner RMDs at age 73, but birth year, account type, employment, plan terms, and first-year timing matter. Roth IRAs generally do not require owner distributions during the owner’s lifetime. Employer plans may allow a still-working participant to delay, while an IRA generally does not use that exception.

Two deadlines can land in one tax year

A first RMD may generally be delayed until April 1 of the following year, but the next annual RMD is still due by December 31 of that same year. Delaying can therefore place two taxable distributions in one calendar year. That is a tax-planning issue, not merely a deadline choice.

Verify the result with the IRS required minimum distribution guidance, the current Publication 590-B, and the account custodian.

Frequently asked questions

Can I withdraw more than the calculated RMD?

Yes. The RMD is a minimum, although an excess generally does not reduce a future year’s RMD.

Can I combine every retirement account?

Not automatically. Aggregation rules differ among IRAs and employer plans. Calculate and satisfy each account under the rules that apply to that account type.
