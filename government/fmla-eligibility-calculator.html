Federal FMLA eligibility works like four interlocking pieces. The employer must be covered; the employee generally needs 12 months of employment; the previous 12 months must include at least 1,250 hours actually worked; and the worksite must have at least 50 employees within 75 miles.

This screen checks those four pieces separately. A result that meets all four is a reason to continue the FMLA analysis—not a final determination that a particular absence is protected.

Gate 1: a covered employer

Public agencies and public or private elementary and secondary schools are generally covered regardless of size. A private employer is generally covered if it employed 50 or more employees for at least 20 workweeks in the current or previous calendar year. Employer coverage and the employee’s 50-within-75 worksite test are related but not identical questions.

Gate 2: twelve months of employment

The 12 months generally do not have to be consecutive. Older periods of employment may be excluded after a break of seven years, with exceptions for military service obligations and certain written agreements. Enter the total months that count under those rules.

Gate 3: 1,250 hours actually worked

The hours test looks backward over the 12 months immediately before leave starts. Vacation, sick leave, holidays, and other paid or unpaid time away generally do not count as hours worked. Airline flight crew employees use special rules, so the tool stops short of evaluating them.

Gate 4: fifty employees within seventy-five miles

This is a worksite test, not simply the company’s worldwide headcount. Remote and jointly employed workers can require a careful worksite analysis based on the office they report to or receive assignments from.

Eligibility is not the whole claim

An eligible employee must still have an FMLA-qualifying reason, give appropriate notice, and provide certification when permitted. Federal FMLA generally provides unpaid, job-protected leave with continuation of group health coverage. State laws, union agreements, and employer policies may be more generous.

Use the U.S. Department of Labor’s FMLA eligibility guidance or contact the Wage and Hour Division for an official explanation.

Frequently asked questions

Do 1,250 paid hours count?

The test generally counts hours actually worked, not every hour for which wages were paid.

What if federal FMLA does not apply?

Check state or local leave laws, disability-accommodation rules, paid-leave programs, and the employer’s own policies.
