Net Worth Tracker

Net worth is the single number that cuts through the noise of daily money. It is what you own minus what you owe, and tracked over time it answers the only question that really matters: is your overall position getting better or worse? A messy month with one improving net worth is fine; a tidy month with a sliding one is not.

This tracker keeps that calculation private. You list your assets and liabilities, it totals them, and it saves the snapshot in your own browser so nothing about your finances ever leaves your device.

Assets

Enter each asset on its own line as "name, amount," for example "Checking, 5000" or "Brokerage, 42000." Include everything with meaningful value: checking and savings, investment and brokerage accounts, retirement balances like a 401(k) or IRA, the estimated market value of real estate you own, vehicles, and any other property worth counting. Two habits keep this honest. Use current market values rather than what you paid, and for retirement accounts use today's balance, not a projected future figure, since the goal is a snapshot of where you stand now, not where you hope to be.

Liabilities

List each debt the same way, "name, amount," such as "Mortgage, 218000" or "Card, 2400." The number that belongs here is the current outstanding balance, not the original loan amount, because only what you still owe reduces your net worth. Common entries include a mortgage, home equity balance, auto loans, student loans, and credit card balances. Small personal debts can be included or left out depending on how granular you want the picture; consistency from one snapshot to the next matters more than capturing every last dollar.

Picture the difference between a homeowner early in a 30-year mortgage and one a decade in. Same house, possibly similar market value, but the second has a far smaller liability, so the equity, and the net worth, is markedly higher. That gap is exactly what tracking over time is built to reveal.

Reading the result over time

The tracker computes total assets, total liabilities, and the difference. A negative result is common and temporary for people early in their financial lives, often because a mortgage or student loans dwarf still-growing assets, and it is not a verdict on anything. The useful signal is direction. A net worth trending upward, even slowly, reflects spending less than you earn while assets appreciate and debts shrink.

  • Entering original loan amounts instead of current payoff balances.
  • Using purchase prices rather than current market values for property.
  • Counting a retirement account's projected future value instead of today's balance.
  • Including some small debts one period and omitting them the next, breaking comparability.
  • Checking too often, so normal market swings read as meaningful change.

How to use this tool

List your assets and liabilities, then calculate and save locally. The tracker shows totals and your net worth, and stores the entry in your browser's local storage so it is waiting for you next visit, without ever being sent to or held on a server. You can clear the saved data anytime with one button. Because everything lives on your own machine, this stays private by design rather than by promise. These figures are estimates you maintain, not financial advice.

Frequently asked questions

How often should I update it?

Quarterly or twice a year suits most people. Daily checking mostly captures market noise and account timing rather than real progress. Picking a consistent rhythm, say January and July, lets genuine trends emerge instead of static.

Should I include my home and car?

Most frameworks do, using current market value as the asset and the outstanding loan as the liability, so the difference is your equity. Some people prefer a liquid net worth that excludes hard-to-sell assets like real estate, which is a perfectly valid alternative view, especially if selling the home is far off.

What counts as a good net worth?

Benchmarks vary so widely by age, income, and cost of living that an outside standard is rarely useful. The more productive question is whether your own number is moving in the right direction from one snapshot to the next.

Where is my data stored?

Only in your browser's local storage on this device. It is not uploaded, synced, or visible to anyone else, which also means it will not appear if you open the tracker on a different device or clear your browser data.
