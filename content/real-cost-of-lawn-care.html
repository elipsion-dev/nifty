Keeping a lawn healthy year-round costs more than most homeowners expect. Between mowing, fertilizing, watering, and controlling weeds and pests, the individual line items add up quickly — yet they rarely appear as a single number anywhere on your budget. This calculator brings them all together so you can see what your lawn actually costs you each year.

Enter the amounts you spend — or expect to spend — for each recurring cost category. The tool works equally well whether you hire a lawn service or do everything yourself. Either way, enter what you pay out of pocket: a mowing service subscription goes in the same field as your own fuel and time budget. The calculator shows your annual total, a monthly average, and a five-year projection so you can plan ahead.

One important note before you start: this calculator covers annual operating costs only. It does not include the one-time purchase or installation costs for your lawn itself — new sod, a landscaping overhaul, or buying a riding mower are not part of this estimate. The caveat is printed clearly in the results, but it is worth keeping in mind as you interpret your numbers.

What each cost category covers

The calculator uses six input fields, each representing a distinct category of recurring lawn expense. Understanding what belongs in each field helps you enter accurate numbers and catch costs you might otherwise forget.

Mowing or lawn service

This is typically the largest single line item. If you hire a lawn service, enter your annual contract or per-visit total. If you mow yourself, enter what you spend on fuel, blades, and any other consumables tied directly to cutting. Labor is not factored here as a dollar cost for DIYers, but the running costs of self-service are still real and worth tracking.

Fertilizer and treatments

A healthy lawn usually requires multiple fertilizer applications per year, often following a spring, summer, and fall schedule. This field covers bagged or liquid fertilizer, pre-emergent and post-emergent herbicide applications, and any professional treatment programs you subscribe to. Costs vary considerably based on lawn size, grass type, and how aggressively you feed.

Seed, soil, and overseeding

Even established lawns need periodic overseeding to fill in thin patches, repair damage, or transition into cooler months. Include grass seed, top dressing soil, and compost or amendments you spread as part of routine maintenance. This is a recurring cost for most lawns, not a one-time fix.

Irrigation and water

If you run an in-ground sprinkler system, your annual water bill during the irrigation season can be substantial. Enter your estimated seasonal water cost here, along with any fees for sprinkler system inspections, backflow testing, startup, or winterization if those are recurring expenses. Homes in dry climates or with large turf areas will see this number climb quickly.

Equipment maintenance and fuel

Lawn equipment requires regular upkeep: oil changes, air filters, spark plugs, blade sharpening, belt replacements, and seasonal tune-ups. Add fuel costs for mowers, trimmers, and blowers over the full year. If you pay for equipment storage or a maintenance contract, include that here as well.

Weed and pest control

This field captures any spending on herbicides, insecticides, grub control, or fungicide treatments not already counted in the fertilizer field. It also covers professional pest control visits specifically targeting lawn-damaging insects or diseases. Some homeowners find this category minimal; others — particularly in regions with grub pressure or persistent weeds — spend more here than they expect.

DIY versus hiring a service

The decision to hire out versus do it yourself affects nearly every category above. Lawn services typically bundle mowing, fertilization, and weed control into package pricing that can be efficient at scale but adds a labor markup. DIY saves on labor but adds equipment costs, time, and the risk of under- or over-treating your lawn. Use your own real numbers in both scenarios to compare them directly — the calculator will show you the annual difference without any assumptions baked in.

How to use this calculator

Enter your annual spending in each field. If you are not sure of an exact number, use your best estimate or check last year's credit card statements and receipts. All calculations run instantly in your browser — nothing you enter is transmitted to any server. The results show your annual total, what that averages out to per month, and what you would spend over five years if costs stay flat. Use the five-year figure when evaluating whether a lawn renovation, irrigation upgrade, or service contract makes financial sense over time.

Frequently asked questions

Why does the calculator exclude the cost of buying a mower or installing new sod?

This tool is designed to show recurring annual operating costs — the money you spend every year just to maintain an existing lawn. A new mower, sod installation, or landscaping project is a capital expense: a one-time cost that is harder to compare on an annual basis. Adding those costs here would make the annual and monthly figures misleading. If you want to factor in a large one-time purchase, divide its cost by the number of years you expect it to last and add that amortized amount to the relevant field manually.

How do I handle costs that vary from year to year?

Enter your best estimate for a typical year. If a cost is genuinely unpredictable — say, you overseed heavily one year and skip it the next — use a two- or three-year average. The five-year projection assumes your current annual cost stays constant, so smoothing out irregular expenses before entering them gives you the most realistic long-term picture.

Is this calculator useful if I hire out everything?

Yes. Simply enter what you pay each service provider in the appropriate field. A full-service lawn care company that handles mowing, fertilization, and weed control in one invoice can be split across the relevant fields, or entered entirely in the mowing or fertilizer line — consistency matters more than perfect categorization. The goal is to capture your total annual outlay so you can see what a well-kept lawn actually costs you each year.
