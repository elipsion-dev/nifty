Small claims court may simplify procedure, but it does not create one nationwide filing deadline. The governing statute of limitations depends on the state, claim type, defendant, accrual rule, and sometimes a pre-suit notice. This worksheet begins only after you have verified that period with an official local source.

Enter the event or accrual date, the verified period, and a personal safety margin. The result gives you a raw arithmetic date and an earlier working target for gathering evidence and filing.

Step 1: identify the legal claim

“Someone owes me money” is not yet a limitation category. A written contract, oral agreement, property damage, personal injury, fraud, security deposit, and claim against a government body can all use different clocks. Start with the legal basis for the requested money.

Step 2: verify when the claim accrued

The clock may begin on the incident date, the breach date, the date payment became due, or the date an injury reasonably should have been discovered. The date you first became frustrated with the dispute is not necessarily the accrual date.

Step 3: look for a shorter notice deadline

Claims against cities, counties, schools, transit agencies, or other public entities often require an administrative claim before a lawsuit. That notice period may be much shorter than the ordinary statute of limitations. Consumer contracts can also require notice, demand, or dispute procedures.

Step 4: confirm small claims jurisdiction

A timely claim can still be filed in the wrong place or for too much money. Check the court’s monetary limit, venue rules, eligible plaintiffs, filing frequency limits, service requirements, and whether the requested remedy can be awarded in small claims.

Step 5: build a filing buffer

Do not plan to file on the calculated last day. Forms can be rejected, electronic systems can close, fees can be missing, and the named defendant can be wrong. The safety-margin target is not a legal deadline; it is an operational deadline designed to leave room to correct a problem.

What this arithmetic omits

Tolling for minority, incapacity, absence, concealment, bankruptcy, emergency orders, or other events can pause or change a limitations period. Weekends and court holidays may affect some deadlines. The calculator only adds tolling days you have independently verified and does not adjust for holidays.

Use your state judiciary or local court’s official self-help pages. Court staff can explain procedure but generally cannot give legal advice. If the deadline is close or uncertain, consult a lawyer or legal-aid organization immediately.

Frequently asked questions

Does the small claims limit determine the filing deadline?

No. The monetary ceiling determines what the court can hear; the statute of limitations determines when a claim becomes time-barred.

Can a demand letter extend the deadline?

Usually you should not assume negotiations or a demand letter stop the clock. Verify tolling under the law that governs the claim.
