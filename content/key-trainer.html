Free Key Trainer game — no sign-up, no download, no catch. One key lights up on an on-screen keyboard, and your job is to press it as fast as you can. It is the most direct way there is to learn where every key lives under your fingers.

The game starts on the home row, then adds the top row, then the whole keyboard as your score climbs. You get thirty seconds to hit as many keys as you can — accurately.

How to play

Press Start game and a key on the on-screen keyboard begins to glow. Press that key on your real keyboard and the next one lights up. Correct presses flash green and score a point; wrong presses flash red and count against your accuracy. After thirty seconds you get your total hits and your accuracy percentage.

What it trains

Touch typing is built on one skill: knowing where each key is without looking. Key Trainer isolates exactly that. Because the target is shown on a keyboard map rather than as plain text, you build a mental picture of the layout — and because the pool grows from the home row outward, you learn the keyboard the same sensible way a typing course teaches it. It is a perfect warm-up before a typing test or a longer game.

Tips for a higher score

Rest your fingers on the home row (a s d f and j k l ;) between presses so every key is a short, known reach. Use the finger that "owns" each key rather than jabbing with one finger. And do not sacrifice accuracy for speed — a missed key costs you percentage points, and clean, confident presses are what build lasting muscle memory.

Frequently asked questions

Is it really free? Yes. No sign-up, no email, no download, and no paywall.

Do you store my scores? No. The game runs entirely in your browser and nothing is uploaded. There is no account and no server keeping a record.

Is this a good way to learn touch typing? It is one of the best drills for learning key locations, which is the foundation of touch typing. Pair it with Word Sprint and the typing speed test to build and measure real speed.

Does it work on a phone? It is built for a physical keyboard. You can tap the on-screen keys on a phone, but the learning benefit comes from a real keyboard.
