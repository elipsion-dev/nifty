The date printed after “Resident Since” is the anchor for many naturalization timelines, but it is not the entire eligibility test. This tool estimates the five- or three-year residence anniversary, counts back USCIS’s 90-day early-filing period, and compares approximate travel with the usual physical-presence threshold.

Think of the output as a document-preparation date: a point at which to audit travel records, marital history when relevant, and state or USCIS-district residence before submitting Form N-400.

The residency clock

The general path requires five years as a lawful permanent resident. Some applicants living in marital union with a qualifying U.S.-citizen spouse use a three-year period. The shorter period carries additional marriage and citizenship requirements that this date calculator cannot verify.

How the 90-day provision works

USCIS permits certain applicants under the general naturalization provisions to file up to 90 calendar days before completing the continuous-residence period. USCIS calculates backward from the day before the anniversary. Filing 90 days early does not make the applicant eligible 90 days early; the full residence period must still be completed before naturalization.

Continuous residence and physical presence are different

Continuous residence asks whether the United States remained your principal dwelling place. Physical presence counts days actually in the country. A general five-year applicant usually needs at least 30 months, or 913 days, of physical presence. A qualifying three-year applicant usually needs at least 18 months, commonly expressed as 548 days.

Travel can change the answer

A single absence of more than six months but less than one year may create a presumption that continuous residence was interrupted. An absence of one year or more generally breaks continuous residence unless a preservation provision applies. Travel-day counting and the evidence needed to overcome a presumption require more detail than a total-days box can capture.

The local residence requirement

An applicant generally must live for at least three months in the state or USCIS district with jurisdiction before filing. A recent move can therefore push the practical filing date later than the 90-day estimate.

Review the USCIS early-filing guidance and your complete travel history before relying on the date.

Frequently asked questions

Does the calculator determine naturalization eligibility?

No. It does not evaluate lawful admission, good moral character, taxes, selective service, English and civics requirements, or special statutory categories.

Should departure and return days count as days in the United States?

USCIS generally counts both the departure day and return day as days of physical presence, but verify the entries against your records.
