Rent paid in cash leaves no bank trail, and "I already paid you for March" is a hard claim to win without paper. A rent receipt ties one specific payment to one specific month, signed by the person who took the money.

For tenants it is proof. For landlords it is a clean record that ends disputes before they start.

Landlord or property manager name

List the name of the person or company actually receiving the payment, whether that is an individual owner, a property management firm, or an authorized agent. If a manager collects on the owner's behalf, the receipt should reflect who took the money so the chain of payment is clear. This is the party whose signature gives the receipt its weight.

Tenant name and property address

The tenant name should match the person on the lease. The property address pins the payment to a specific unit, which matters for landlords who own several rentals and for tenants who later need to show where they lived. Include the unit or apartment number when there is one. A receipt that names the tenant and the address is often accepted as informal proof of residency for things like a library card or a utility transfer, though the requesting organization decides what it accepts.

Amount paid and rental period

These two fields work as a pair, and getting them right is the whole point of the receipt. The amount is what was actually handed over; the rental period is what that money covers. Writing "May 1–31, 2026" rather than just "May" removes any ambiguity about which month a payment applies to, which becomes important when a tenant pays late, pays partially, or pays two months at once. If the payment is partial, note the remaining balance so both sides agree on what is still owed.

Payment method and date received

Record how the money moved: cash, check, money order, or electronic transfer. For a check or money order, jotting the number gives both parties a second reference point. The date received is when the landlord actually took the payment, which is the date that matters for judging whether rent arrived on time under the lease.

  • Write the exact period covered, not just the month name.
  • Note any remaining balance on partial payments.
  • Record the check or money-order number when applicable.
  • Issue a receipt every time, especially for cash.
  • Common mistake: dating the receipt the day it was written rather than the day payment was received.

Frequently asked questions

Are landlords required to give rent receipts?

It varies by state. Some require a written receipt for cash payments, and a few require one for any payment on request. Check the landlord-tenant rules where the property is located. Even where it is optional, issuing receipts consistently protects both parties.

How long should I keep rent receipts?

Tenants are generally well served keeping them at least a year past move-out, in case a deposit dispute arises. Landlords reporting rental income usually keep payment records for several years in line with their tax record-keeping. A tax professional can advise on the right retention period for your situation.

Can a rent receipt prove I live somewhere?

Sometimes. A receipt showing your name and the property address is accepted by some agencies, schools, and utilities as informal evidence of residency. Others insist on a signed lease or official mail. Whether it works depends entirely on the organization asking.

Is a rent receipt the same as a lease?

No. A lease sets the terms of the tenancy; a receipt only confirms that a particular payment was made. They serve different purposes, and a thorough rental file keeps both.
