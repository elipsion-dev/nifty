Two invoices with the same number is how a client pays one bill and assumes the other is settled too. A gap in the sequence is how an accountant spends an afternoon hunting for an invoice that never existed. The fix is boring and effective: a consistent numbering scheme you never have to think about.

This generator hands you a clean block of numbers in the format you choose, ready to paste into whatever you bill with.

Prefix

The prefix is the human-readable label at the front of every number. Good choices are short and meaningful: your initials, a business abbreviation, a client code, or simply "INV." A prefix makes invoices easy to spot in a shared inbox or bank statement and lets you run separate sequences for different purposes, for example "RET-" for retainers and "PROJ-" for project work. Keep it consistent once you choose it, because changing prefixes midstream is how duplicate-looking numbers sneak in.

Starting number

This sets where the sequence begins. If you are brand new, starting at 1001 rather than 1 is a common cosmetic choice that makes a young business look more established and avoids tiny numbers like INV-1. If you are continuing from an existing system, set this to the number right after your last issued invoice. Last invoice was INV-1047? Start at 1048, and the new block picks up without a gap or overlap.

Count

Count is how many numbers to generate in one go. Producing a block in advance, say enough for a quarter, lets you assign numbers as you bill without tracking the last one used by hand. This is especially handy for spreadsheet-based invoicing, where there is no software to auto-increment for you. Generate a realistic batch; you can always make more later starting from the next number.

Include the current date

Turning on the date option inserts today's date in compact numeric form between the prefix and the sequential number, producing something like INV-20260626-001. Date-stamped numbers let you tell at a glance which period an invoice belongs to, which speeds up filing and reconciliation. If you prefer a cleaner look or your accounting software dislikes long numbers, leave it off and rely on the sequence alone.

  • Keep the prefix short and stable once chosen.
  • Continue an existing sequence from last issued + 1.
  • Make sure every number is unique within your records.
  • Decide early whether you want date-based or pure sequential numbers.
  • Common mistake: running two systems at once and creating overlapping numbers.

Frequently asked questions

Do invoice numbers have to be sequential?

Sequential numbering is common practice and simplifies record-keeping, but most jurisdictions do not strictly require consecutive numbers for general business. Some tax authorities and accounting standards do recommend or require unbroken sequences for audit purposes, so check the rules where you operate if you are unsure.

Can I reset the sequence each year?

Yes, and many businesses do, often folding the year into the prefix like 2026-001. Annual resets make it easy to see which year an invoice belongs to. Continuous numbering works equally well. Either is fine as long as each number stays unique in your records.

I have already issued invoices. How do I continue cleanly?

Set the starting number to the next number after your most recent invoice. The generator produces a continuous sequence from there, so your numbering stays gap-free and duplicate-free as you migrate or switch tools.

Can I generate random instead of sequential numbers?

Sequential and date-based numbering are easiest to reconcile and are recommended for most billing. If you have a specific reason to avoid a predictable sequence, you can still use unique numbers, but keep a master list so you never repeat one.
