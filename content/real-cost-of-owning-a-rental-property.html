Rental property can generate real monthly income — or quietly drain your bank account every month. The difference comes down to whether the rent you collect actually covers the mortgage payment, taxes, insurance, maintenance, and management costs stacked against it. This calculator helps you see that net figure before you sign anything.

Enter your purchase details, expected rent, and operating costs, and the calculator returns your projected monthly and annual cash flow. The result can be negative — that is not an error. Negative cash flow is a legitimate outcome that some investors accept when they believe appreciation or tax benefits will compensate over time. This tool shows you the cash flow picture clearly so you can make that judgment with open eyes.

How rental property cash flow actually works

Cash flow is simple in concept: the money coming in minus the money going out each month. In practice, the "money going out" has more line items than most first-time landlords expect. The calculator walks through each of them so nothing is hiding in a rounding error.

Financing: down payment and mortgage payment

Your down payment reduces the amount you borrow, which directly reduces your monthly mortgage payment. A larger down payment means less debt service every month — which makes cash flow easier to achieve — but it also means more capital tied up in a single asset. The mortgage payment is calculated from your loan amount, interest rate, and term using standard amortization. In the early years of a 30-year loan, the vast majority of each payment goes to interest rather than principal, which matters when you think about total return but does not change the cash going out the door each month.

Income side: rent and vacancy allowance

The calculator starts with your expected monthly rent, then applies a vacancy allowance to arrive at effective annual rent. A 6% vacancy factor, for example, represents roughly three weeks of empty unit per year. Actual vacancy rates vary by market, property type, tenant turnover history, and how quickly you can re-rent. In high-demand urban markets vacancy might run lower; in slower markets or with frequent tenant changes it can run significantly higher. The effective rent number — gross rent reduced by your vacancy assumption — is what the calculator uses on the income side of the ledger.

Property tax

Property tax is assessed locally and varies enormously by state, county, and municipality. What matters for cash flow is the annual dollar amount, not the rate in isolation, because the rate applies to an assessed value that may differ from purchase price and can reset after a sale depending on your jurisdiction. Verify the current tax bill with the county assessor before closing — do not rely on the seller's figure if there has been a recent assessment change.

Insurance

A landlord policy (also called a dwelling fire policy) covers the structure and your liability as a property owner but does not cover the tenant's belongings — that is their renters insurance to carry. Premiums depend on the dwelling's replacement cost, location, age, construction type, and the coverage limits and deductibles you choose. Get a quote specific to the address rather than estimating from a neighbor's premium; flood and earthquake coverage, if needed, are separate policies with separate costs.

Maintenance and repairs reserve

Maintenance costs are real even when nothing is broken yet. A reasonable annual reserve accounts for routine upkeep plus the statistical likelihood of an appliance failure, plumbing issue, or roof repair in any given year. Common rules of thumb — such as setting aside 1% of purchase price annually — vary widely depending on the property's age, condition, and what systems are approaching end of life. A newer home with recent mechanicals may need less; an older property with an aging HVAC and original windows may need considerably more. The reserve entered here is a planning figure; actual spending will be lumpy across years.

Property management fee

If you hire a property management company to handle tenant placement, rent collection, maintenance coordination, and legal compliance, the fee is typically calculated as a percentage of collected rent. Rates vary by market and scope of service — some managers charge separately for leasing fees or maintenance markups on top of the base percentage. If you plan to self-manage, entering zero here shows you the full potential cash flow; just remember that self-management has a real time cost that does not show up in this model.

Cash flow versus total return

Monthly cash flow is not the same as total return on your investment. This calculator shows you one slice: the net of rent income against financing and operating costs, before income taxes on rental income. It does not model principal paydown (equity building through amortization), property appreciation, depreciation deductions on your tax return, or the opportunity cost of the down payment capital. A property with negative monthly cash flow may still generate a positive total return when all of those factors are included — or it may not. Separating the cash flow picture from the full return picture is useful precisely because they can point in different directions.

Frequently asked questions

Is negative cash flow always a sign that a deal is bad?

Not necessarily. Some investors intentionally accept modest negative cash flow when they project strong appreciation, significant depreciation tax benefits, or rapid rent growth in the market. The key is that negative cash flow should be a deliberate choice with a clear thesis, not a surprise. This calculator surfaces the number early so you can decide whether the math pencils out for your situation rather than discovering it after closing.

Why does the calculator show cash flow before taxes and depreciation?

Tax treatment of rental income and depreciation deductions varies by your individual income level, filing status, whether you qualify as an active participant or real estate professional under IRS rules, and whether your losses can offset other income in a given year. Those variables are specific to your tax situation and ideally reviewed with a CPA. Showing pre-tax cash flow keeps this tool general and avoids giving you a number that looks precise but may be wrong for your circumstances.

How should I handle capital expenditures like a new roof or HVAC replacement?

The maintenance and repairs input in this calculator is intended for an annual reserve that covers both routine upkeep and a portion of longer-cycle capital replacements. In practice, a new roof or HVAC unit is a large one-time outlay that may not hit in any given year but will eventually arrive. Many investors model capital expenditures separately — estimating the remaining useful life of major systems and dividing the replacement cost over that horizon — to avoid understating true operating costs. If your property has aging major systems, consider increasing your reserve figure to reflect the realistic replacement timeline.
