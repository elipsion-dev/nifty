Equipment

Name each item clearly enough to identify it on its own. "Generator" is weak; "Honda EU2200i portable generator" is strong. Include the make and model, because that is what an insurer, an auditor, or a replacement quote will key on. For items that come in quantity, like twenty identical folding tables, you can group them in one row and note the count, but anything with a serial number deserves its own line.

Serial number

This is the field that proves a specific item was yours, not just that you owned something like it. Record the manufacturer's serial number or your own internal asset tag. Serial numbers are what police enter into recovery databases after a theft and what insurers use to verify a claim. Photograph the serial plate while you are at it; the photo is a useful companion to the written record.

Location

Note where each item lives or who it is assigned to: a building, a room, a vehicle, a job site, or a staff member's name. Location turns a flat list into something operational. When equipment moves between sites or people, updating this column tells you instantly what should be where, which speeds up both daily accountability and any post-loss reconciliation.

Value

Enter an estimated current or replacement value. Decide up front whether you are recording what you paid, what the item is worth used today, or what it would cost to replace new, and stay consistent across the list. For insurance purposes, replacement cost is usually the most relevant figure. A consistent value column lets you total your exposure and check it against your coverage limits. If a single item is worth more than a typical policy's per-item cap, that is a flag to ask your insurer about scheduling it separately rather than discovering the gap after a loss.

  • Give each serialized item its own row.
  • Record make and model, not just a generic name.
  • Photograph serial plates to back up the written record.
  • Pick one valuation basis and apply it consistently.
  • Common mistake: letting the file go stale after equipment moves or is retired.

How to use this generator

Add a row per item and enter the equipment name, serial number or asset tag, location, and estimated value. Use Add Row to keep going, then Download CSV to save the file. Open it in a spreadsheet to sort by location, filter by value, or update it over time. Everything happens in your browser; nothing you enter is uploaded or stored by this site. Keep a copy somewhere off-site so a loss at one location does not take the inventory with it.

Frequently asked questions

How is this different from an asset register?

An equipment inventory tracks physical items by description, serial number, and location. A business asset register adds financial detail like purchase date, depreciation, and book value. This tool covers the physical side; the Business Asset Register tool on this site covers the accounting side. Many businesses keep both.

Should I include leased or rented equipment?

It depends on the goal. For insurance, you generally list only what you own. For an operational record of everything your team uses, leased items belong too. A simple fix is to add an "ownership" column in the spreadsheet after downloading so owned and leased gear stay distinguishable.

How often should I update it?

Any time equipment is bought, moved, disposed of, or changes value meaningfully. Many organizations also reconcile the whole list once a year, often at fiscal year-end or before renewing equipment insurance. Frequent small updates beat one annual scramble.

Will this file work with my accounting software?

The CSV opens in any spreadsheet and can usually be reshaped to match an import template. Check your software's required columns and reorder or rename as needed before importing.
