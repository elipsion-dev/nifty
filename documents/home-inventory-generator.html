Item

Name each belonging or group of similar belongings. High-value items, electronics, appliances, jewelry, artwork, power tools, deserve individual lines with as much detail as you can give. Lower-value things can be grouped sensibly: "kitchen cookware, assorted" with an estimated lump value is far better than skipping them entirely. The goal is coverage of what you actually own, not a museum catalog.

Room

Organizing by room is the trick that makes the whole project manageable and keeps you from missing things. Work through the house one space at a time, including the garage, attic, basement, and any storage unit. The room field also helps an adjuster understand a claim, since losses are often localized, a kitchen fire, a flooded basement, and a room-tagged inventory lets you quickly pull the affected items.

Serial or notes

Use this field for the details that prove a specific item was yours and establish its quality: serial and model numbers for electronics and appliances, brand and materials for furniture, and identifying notes for jewelry or collectibles. These specifics matter because replacement coverage often pays for a like-kind item, and "55-inch OLED, 2024 model" supports a very different payout than "TV." Photos or short videos of each room pair well with these notes.

Replacement value

Replacement value is what it would cost to buy a new equivalent today, not what you paid years ago and not the used resale price. For ordinary goods, a quick check of a current retail price is enough. For antiques, art, fine jewelry, or anything unusual, a professional appraisal gives a figure that holds up better for insurance. Totaling this column tells you whether your coverage limits actually match what you own.

  • Go room by room so nothing gets skipped.
  • List high-value items individually; group the small stuff.
  • Capture serial and model numbers wherever they exist.
  • Value at today's replacement cost, not purchase price.
  • Common mistake: storing the only copy of the inventory inside the home it documents.

How to use this generator

Add a row per item or category and enter the item, room, any serial or notes, and a replacement value. Use Add Row to keep going, then Download CSV. Open it in a spreadsheet to total values by room or sort by value to find your biggest exposures. Everything happens in your browser; nothing you enter is uploaded or stored by this site. Crucially, save a copy somewhere outside your home, cloud storage or an email to yourself, so it survives the very events it is meant for.

Frequently asked questions

Should I list everything or just expensive things?

Listing everything is most accurate, but starting with high-value items, electronics, appliances, jewelry, art, furniture, captures most of the dollars for far less effort. Group inexpensive miscellaneous items by category with an estimated total rather than itemizing each spoon.

How do I estimate replacement value?

Use today's cost to buy a new equivalent, not what you originally paid or the used value. A current retailer price works for common items. For antiques, collectibles, or fine jewelry, a professional appraisal produces a more defensible figure for insurance.

Where should I keep the file?

Somewhere other than the home it covers, so it remains accessible after a fire, flood, or theft. Cloud storage, an email to yourself, or a copy held by someone you trust all work. Photos or a walkthrough video of each room make a strong companion to the written list.

How often should I update it?

After any significant purchase, a move, a renovation, or an inheritance of valuable items. A once-a-year review, perhaps when your policy renews, keeps the totals and your coverage in step.
