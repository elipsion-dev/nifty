A card swipe leaves a trail; a stack of twenties does not. The instant cash changes hands at a yard sale, a market stall, or a club fundraiser, the only record is the one you choose to write. A printable receipt is that record, simple proof that this person paid that person this amount, for this reason, on this day.

Unlike an invoice, which asks for payment, a receipt confirms payment already happened.

Received from and received by

"Received from" is the person who paid; "received by" is the person or business that took the money. Naming both fixes the direction of the transaction so there is no later confusion about who owes whom. When you are collecting on behalf of an organization, a sports league, a church, a class, put the organization in "received by" and have the authorized person sign, which keeps the receipt tied to the group rather than an individual's pocket.

Amount and payment for

The amount is what was actually handed over; write it clearly and consider spelling it out to prevent later alteration. "Payment for" is the description, and a specific one does real work: "deposit on 2014 utility trailer" or "June soccer registration, two players" tells anyone reading exactly what the money settled. A vague "services" line invites the disputes a receipt is supposed to prevent.

Payment method

Record how the money moved, cash, check, money order, or electronic transfer, because the method can matter if a question comes up later. For a check or money order, noting the number gives both parties a second reference point that ties the receipt to a bank record. For cash, the receipt itself is the only record, which is exactly why issuing one matters most in that case.

Date and receipt number

The date pins the transaction in time. The receipt number is your tracking handle: the generator assigns one automatically, but you can override it to fit your own sequence. Consistent receipt numbers let you keep an orderly run of payments over a season or a year and make it easy to reference a specific receipt later, the same discipline a business applies to invoices.

  • Name both the payer and the recipient.
  • Describe specifically what the payment was for.
  • Note the check or money-order number when relevant.
  • Keep receipt numbers in a consistent sequence.
  • Common mistake: issuing no receipt at all for cash, the one case where it matters most.

Frequently asked questions

Is a handwritten or printed receipt legally valid?

A written receipt, printed or handwritten, is generally valid as evidence that a payment occurred. There is usually no requirement that it take a particular form or come from specific software. What counts is that it accurately records the details and is signed by the recipient. Where enforceability might matter, keep a copy and have both parties sign.

What payment methods should I list?

List what the payer actually used, cash, check, money order, or electronic transfer. Recording the method helps if there is ever a dispute about how payment was made. For checks, adding the check number gives both sides an extra reference.

Can I use this for business receipts?

Yes, for simple transactions, cash sales, informal service payments, and organizational collections. Higher-volume businesses will usually find dedicated point-of-sale or accounting software more efficient and better integrated with their books. Where tax or legal documentation rules apply, confirm the format meets those requirements.

Do I need to give a receipt for every payment?

You are not always required to, but it is good practice, especially for cash, partial payments, and deposits. A receipt protects the payer with proof and protects the recipient with a clear record of what was settled and when.
