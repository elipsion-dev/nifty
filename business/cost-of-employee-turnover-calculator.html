When someone quits, the resignation letter shows up in a single moment but the bill arrives in installments. Vacancy cost

When a seat sits empty, the work either goes undone or lands on everyone else. Enter how many days the role typically stays open and a daily estimate of the productivity lost while it does. That estimate can take a few forms: the value of output not produced, the overtime you pay other staff to cover, or the share of a manager's day swallowed by patching the gap. Vacancy length swings widely with role complexity and the labor market, and in a tight market it is often the most underestimated piece because the search drags on far longer than planned.

Recruiting and hiring costs

These are the direct, out-of-pocket costs of finding the replacement: job board postings, recruiter or agency fees, background checks, drug screens, and the interview hours valued at each interviewer's loaded cost. External recruiter fees can run a meaningful slice of first-year salary, while a referral or direct applicant costs a fraction of that. Your number depends on how you hire and how hot the market is for the skill set.

Training cost

Training covers what you spend getting the new hire to baseline competence: the trainer's or manager's time, onboarding materials, required certifications, and any outside courses. Keep this separate from the productivity drag of the early weeks, which the calculator captures on its own. Bundling them double-counts and overstates the total.

Ramp-up period and productivity loss

Almost no one is fully productive on day one. Enter how many months it takes someone in this role to reach full output and the percentage of productivity lost during that climb relative to a seasoned employee. This is frequently the largest single component, especially for roles built on judgment, relationships, or institutional knowledge. A six-month ramp at 40 percent reduced productivity on a well-paid role can dwarf the recruiting fees everyone focuses on. The longer and steeper the ramp, the more a preventable departure actually costs.

  • Counting only recruiter fees and ignoring the ramp-up drag, which is usually larger.
  • Underestimating vacancy length in a tight labor market.
  • Forgetting the lost institutional knowledge that walks out with a tenured employee.
  • Treating turnover as a fixed cost of business rather than a number worth lowering.

How to use this calculator

Enter the role's annual salary, your vacancy duration and daily productivity-loss estimate, direct hiring costs, training expense, and the ramp-up parameters. The result shows the estimated total cost of one turnover event and what it represents as a percentage of annual salary. Run it for your highest-turnover role first; that is usually where retention spending pays back fastest. Everything is calculated in your browser; nothing you enter is uploaded or stored.

Frequently asked questions

Why do published turnover-cost estimates vary so much?

Because different frameworks include different components and make different assumptions about vacancy length, ramp-up depth, and what counts as a direct versus indirect cost. Some headline figures fold in hard-to-measure morale effects; others count only invoices. This calculator reports what your inputs imply, which is more useful than any one-size-fits-all multiple of salary.

Which roles carry the highest replacement cost?

Roles with long ramp-up periods, high salaries, specialized skills, or direct ownership of customer relationships. A salesperson who leaves with a book of accounts, or a senior technician who holds knowledge no one documented, can cost the business far more than the recruiting line suggests, because the lost output and lost relationships continue long after the seat is refilled.

How do businesses actually lower these costs?

The biggest win is preventing the departure: competitive pay, clear advancement, decent management, and realistic workloads. When turnover does happen, a documented onboarding process, cross-trained staff who can cover the gap, and a warm candidate pipeline all shrink the vacancy and ramp-up periods that drive most of the total.
